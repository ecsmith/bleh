GivJoy×ProtoHack

When was the last time you made someone's day?

The Pitch

Sarah, a marketing specialist posed the above prompt in her initial pitch for the idea of GivJoy at the beginning of ProtoHack. This question really resonated with me and I decided to join their team and help them solve their problem.

What we made — GivJoy

Givjoy is a mobile application for allowing a person to bring joy to their friends. GivJoy does this by creating a marketplace for wherein a joy-sender can employ a person with a joy-giving, creative talents to bring joy to their friends. GivJoy was created at ProtoHack, a code-free hackathon, in 9 hours.

Uncertainties

Going into ProtoHack I had no idea what to expect. As a web developer, the idea of a strictly no coding hackthon seemed backwards to me. If there's been something I've learned about the design world it's that designers get passionate about their designs–I was fearful of how this would come into play in this space.

―To say the least I was anxious about what the day would bring. The turnout of ProtoHack mostly consisted of buisiness development professionals, UX designers and marketers. They gave us the morning of ProtoHack to build teams and comes up with ideas for a product. Many passionate individuals even took to the stage to pitch their ideas hoping to find the talent needed to act on them.

About ProtoHack

ProtoHack exists to show non-coders that they can create something amazing from nothing. It empowers and enables the non-technical community with the tools, knowledge and connections that will help bring their idea to life and visually communicate it through prototyping. Competitors may work in teams (up to 5) or solo. You have 9 hours to prototype your idea and then you present it on stage.

My Role in our Team

Our ProtoHack team consisted of 5 individuals–3 Buisiness Development experts and 1 other UX Designer besides myself. I was by far the most junior person in the team–Most of the others had been in their respective industries for 5+ years. I wanted to pull my own weight in the team and prove myself.

Our Initial Steps

ProtoHack has begun! In the beginning each member of the team had various assumptions about what we were going to build. Our initial steps in the process were establishing ground rules, aligning the team, creating a schedule for the day and identifying our problem. I proposed we try an activity similar to an affinity diagraming exercise to flesh out our assumptions. It was this exercise that helped us hone in our intial problem statement:

Our Users have trouble finding a unique way to spread joy to friends & family. Its time consuming to find unique gifts for them.

The activity led us to the idea of a mobile marketplace and we hypothesized 3 different user archetypes for the platform. We decided to identify 2 groups, Joy-senders and joy-receivers on the endpoints of the application. The third archetype we hypothesized were the creatives who were the basis for the marketplace. The creatives are people who have some sort of creative talent to bring joy to other people. We decided that the platform would work based around these 3 groups.

Wouldn't it be great if there was a platform to give joy to your loved ones?

The affinity mapping exercise also revealed that we would need to do more research to validate our hypotheses. Together we needed to validate with real people whether the joy bringing platform would be a viable idea. We needed to figure out "What incites joy in people?", "Should gift-giving be part of our solution?" and "What motivation do people have in giving joy?"

We decided that the scope of the hackthon would be designing a user flow for the joy-sender. Much of the research we needed to do had to identify what was important to our user flow on the side of the joy-sender.

Research

Myself and the other UX Designer took to the streets to get some quick validation of our concept. The questions we decided to ask focused on motivations and needs of the joy-sender.

  1. Tell us about the last time you gave a gift to someone that wasn't a special occasion. What was their reaction?
  2. How did you know what to get that person? Where did you get it from?
  3. What was the most memorable gift you have ever recieved not for an occasion? Why was it so special?

We managed to interview 7 people in the time we scheduled for research. After directly returning to our team we were able to sythesize a few key insights from our interviews:

  1. We found that in almost all cases when we asked the participant to recall their most memorable gift that the gift was not a purely physical gift. Most of the time the most salient part of the memory was the person or emotion associated with the gift.
  2. In almost all cases the most memorable gift invloved some element of surprise.
  3. When asked about their motivation behind sending a gift to a loved one–much of the time the gift was given to console them.

Next Steps

I think the next steps in our process would be to validate our design with users and either iterate the joy-sender design or explore the application views of the other two personas.

As a junior designer I intially felt very insecure in a team of more experienced folks. However after ProtoHack I discovered that junior designers have such fresh experiences that we bring to a project. I've learned to be more comfortable and confident in my skills.

I also learned that research is ALWAYS possible within ANY organization and within ANY time frame. We did research in a 7 hour project with no budget and it was infinately helpful in understanding our users.
